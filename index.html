• The Problem Our Mission

The USDA defines food insecurity as "the limited or uncertain availability of nutritionally adequate and safe foods or limited or uncertain ability to acquire acceptable foods in socially acceptable ways."

In 2018 alone, more than 14.3 million U.S households suffered from food insecurity (Feeding America). With limited resources and a limited food budget to spend, a 2014 study found that 79% of food insecure households resorted to inexpensive, unhealthy food, as they sacrificed diet quality over quantity. An additional 31% of study participants also mentioned "[having] to choose between food and education" (Feeding America).

Not only does the lack of a stable and nutritious food source contribute to an overall poorer health, but it has also proven to have adverse effects on the development of young children, including "increased hospitalizations, [...] iron deficiency, developmental risk and behavior problems, primarily aggression, anxiety, depression, and attention deficit disorder" (American Psychological Association).

But what if families didn't have to choose between food and education? What if they could grow their own fresh produce from the comfort of their homes, while their children could simulatanously learn about hydroponics?

Food shouldn't have to be a luxury and we're here to fix that.
